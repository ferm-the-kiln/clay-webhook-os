# CEO / Founder

## Role

Owns the company's vision, strategy, and survival. Accountable to the
board and investors for growth, capital efficiency, and market position.
In early-stage (Seed to Series B), often deeply involved in sales, product,
and hiring. In growth-stage, shifts to strategy, fundraising, board
management, and culture. The ultimate economic buyer — but also the
hardest to reach and the most skeptical of cold outreach.

## What They Own

- Company vision, strategy, and market positioning
- Board relationships, fundraising, and investor narrative
- Capital allocation: where to invest, where to cut
- Executive hiring and organizational design
- Strategic partnerships and M&A
- Company culture, values, and operating cadence
- Key customer relationships (especially in early stage)
- Public-facing narrative: press, events, thought leadership

## How They're Measured

- **Revenue growth rate**: ARR growth, net revenue retention
- **Capital efficiency**: burn multiple (net burn / net new ARR)
- **Runway**: months of cash remaining, path to profitability
- **Market position**: category leadership signals, analyst recognition
- **Team quality**: ability to attract and retain A-players
- **Board confidence**: fund-raising ability, valuation trajectory
- **Customer metrics**: logo retention, NPS, expansion revenue
- **Competitive moat**: defensibility of market position

## What Keeps Them Up at Night

- Running out of runway before hitting the next milestone
- Losing a key executive or a critical cluster of talent
- Market narrative shifting away from their category
- Competitor raising a massive round and outspending them
- Board losing confidence in the growth trajectory
- Making the wrong strategic bet that wastes 6-12 months
- Not finding product-market fit (early stage) or losing it (growth stage)
- Capital markets tightening while they need to raise

## Buying Triggers

- Preparing for next fundraise and need to show operational leverage
- Board pressure to improve a specific metric (efficiency, growth, retention)
- Competitive threat that requires rapid capability deployment
- Entering a new market where they lack internal expertise
- Post-fundraise with fresh capital and aggressive growth mandate
- Key executive departure that creates a capability gap
- Pivoting strategy and need to move faster than internal build allows
- Investor or board member makes a specific recommendation or introduction

## Language That Resonates

- "Capital efficiency" / "do more with the team you have"
- "Competitive moat" / "defensible advantage"
- "We helped [similar-stage company] get to [milestone] in [timeframe]"
- "Board-ready metrics" / "investor-grade reporting"
- "Time-to-impact" / "live in weeks, not months"
- "Founder-to-founder" / peer-level credibility
- "What are you optimizing for this quarter?" (shows you listen first)

## Language to Avoid

- "Just wanted to introduce myself" (no value, wastes time)
- "Synergy" / "leverage" / "holistic" (corporate fluff)
- "Thought leadership" as the pitch (they are the thought leader)
- "Long implementation timeline" (they can't afford to wait)
- "Enterprise features you'll grow into" (patronizing for early stage)
- Feature-level pitches without connecting to business outcomes
- "I know you're busy, but..." (everyone says this)

## Best Outreach Channels

1. **Warm intro** — from a board member, investor, or peer founder (by far
   the highest conversion; cold outreach to CEOs has very low hit rates)
2. **Email** — only if extremely personalized and value-dense; 2-3 sentences
3. **Twitter/X** — many founders are active here; engage authentically first
4. **Events** — founder dinners, investor events, small-format conferences
5. **LinkedIn** — last resort for cold; works better after they've seen
   your content or your company name elsewhere

## Best Opening Angles

1. **The capital efficiency angle** — Reference their funding stage
   and growth targets. Frame your product as something that delivers
   output of X headcount at a fraction of the cost. In 2024-2026,
   capital efficiency is the dominant board conversation. Connect
   directly to burn multiple or runway extension.

2. **The competitive positioning angle** — Reference a specific
   competitive move (competitor's funding, launch, or hire) and
   position your product as something that helps them respond or
   leap ahead. CEOs are intensely competitive and track every move
   their rivals make.

3. **The founder peer proof angle** — Lead with a specific founder
   at a comparable company who uses your product and the result they
   achieved. CEOs trust peer founders more than any marketing claim.
   Name-drop only with permission and only when genuinely comparable.

## Common Objections

- **"Send me a one-pager."** — This is often a polite deflection. Send
  the one-pager, but make it genuinely useful (not a brochure). Include
  a specific, quantified case study and a clear next step. Follow up
  once, then let it breathe.
- **"Talk to my [VP/Head of X]."** — This is actually a win. Take the
  intro warmly. The CEO just gave you an internal champion with a
  mandate to evaluate. Close the VP, then loop the CEO back in for
  final sign-off.
- **"We're not spending on anything right now."** — Reframe around
  cost savings or revenue acceleration, not new spend. Show how your
  product pays for itself within one quarter. If they truly can't
  spend, ask when budget opens up and set a timed follow-up.
- **"I need to see this work first."** — Offer a no-risk pilot with
  a defined success metric and timeline. CEOs respect confidence
  in your own product. Make the pilot dead simple to start.
- **"We're building this internally."** — Ask how far along they are
  and what it's costing. Share the common pattern: companies build
  v1, realize maintenance cost exceeds vendor cost, then switch.
  Don't argue — plant the seed and follow up in 3 months.
