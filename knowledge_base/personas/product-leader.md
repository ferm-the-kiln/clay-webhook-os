# VP / Head of Product

## Role

Owns the product strategy and roadmap. Reports to CEO or CPO. Responsible
for deciding what gets built, why, and in what order. Lives at the
intersection of customer needs, business goals, and engineering capacity.
Constantly negotiating tradeoffs between stakeholders.

## What They Own

- Product roadmap and prioritization framework
- Feature discovery, validation, and go-to-market alignment
- User research and customer feedback loops
- Product-market fit measurement and iteration
- Cross-functional alignment with Engineering, Design, Sales, and Marketing
- Competitive intelligence and differentiation strategy
- Product analytics — activation, engagement, retention metrics
- Time-to-value optimization for new users and features

## How They're Measured

- **User engagement**: DAU/MAU ratio, session depth, feature adoption rates
- **Time-to-value**: how fast new users reach their "aha moment"
- **Retention**: cohort retention curves, churn reduction
- **NPS / CSAT**: customer satisfaction and likelihood to recommend
- **Feature velocity**: discovery-to-ship cycle time
- **Revenue impact**: product-led growth metrics, expansion revenue
- **Competitive win rate**: product cited as reason for winning deals

## What Keeps Them Up at Night

- Building features nobody uses (waste of scarce engineering cycles)
- Losing competitive deals because of a missing capability
- User churn they can't diagnose or reverse
- Roadmap hijacked by loudest stakeholders instead of data
- Time-to-value too slow — users abandon before reaching activation
- Engineering bottleneck blocking the highest-impact features
- Not enough signal from user research to make confident bets
- Balancing tech debt paydown against feature delivery pressure

## Buying Triggers

- User engagement or retention metrics plateau or decline
- Competitor launches a feature that customers start asking about
- Sales team escalates lost deals citing specific product gaps
- Board sets aggressive growth targets that require product-led motion
- New market or segment entry that requires rapid product adaptation
- User research reveals a systemic UX friction they can't solve internally
- Engineering capacity freed up after a major milestone — looking for leverage

## Language That Resonates

- "Reduce time-to-value" / "accelerate activation"
- "Data-driven prioritization" / "evidence-based roadmap"
- "Your users are already trying to do this — we make it native"
- "Ship faster without compromising quality"
- "Here's what [comparable product] measured after implementing this"
- "Differentiation your competitors can't easily copy"
- "Frees your PM team to focus on strategy, not operations"

## Language to Avoid

- "Feature-rich" / "full-featured" (they care about outcomes, not features)
- "We integrate with everything" (vague, unverifiable)
- "Your users will love it" (presumptuous — they do the user research)
- "Set it and forget it" (product leaders iterate constantly)
- Technical jargon without business context
- Anything that implies their product is broken or inferior

## Best Outreach Channels

1. **Email** — concise, outcome-focused, reference their product specifically
2. **LinkedIn** — engage on product strategy content they share
3. **Product communities** (Lenny's Newsletter, Mind the Product, CPO forums)
4. **Conference connections** — product conferences are high-signal environments

## Best Opening Angles

1. **The user engagement angle** — Reference a specific user experience
   in their product (you tried it yourself) and connect it to a
   measurable improvement your product enables. Product leaders respect
   people who actually use their product.

2. **The competitive gap angle** — Point to a capability a direct
   competitor recently shipped and connect it to how your product
   helps them respond faster. Be specific — vague competitive FUD
   backfires with product leaders.

3. **The roadmap leverage angle** — Frame your product as something
   that gives their engineering team capacity back for higher-value
   features. Quantify in sprint weeks saved, not dollars.

## Common Objections

- **"This isn't on our roadmap."** — Don't push for immediate adoption.
  Ask about their prioritization framework and where this type of
  capability would rank. Offer data that helps them make the case.
- **"We'd need to validate this with users first."** — Perfect. Offer
  to support a user research sprint or provide a sandbox for user
  testing. Meet them in their process.
- **"Our engineering team is fully allocated."** — Reframe around
  engineering time saved, not added. Show how your product reduces
  scope on something already on the roadmap.
- **"How does this affect our product experience?"** — Be ready with
  UX specifics: what changes for the end user, what stays the same,
  customization options, white-label capabilities.
- **"I need to align with my CTO on this."** — Offer a joint brief
  that covers both product impact and technical integration. Make it
  easy for them to champion internally.
