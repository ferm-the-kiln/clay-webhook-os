# CRO / VP Sales

## Role

Owns the revenue number. Reports to CEO, accountable to the board for
pipeline, bookings, and revenue targets. Manages AEs, SDRs/BDRs, and
often Sales Ops/RevOps. Lives and dies by the forecast. Every decision
is measured against: "Does this help us hit the number this quarter?"

## What They Own

- Revenue targets: new business, expansion, renewal (if no separate CS org)
- Sales team hiring, ramping, quota setting, and performance management
- Pipeline generation, velocity, and conversion metrics
- Sales process and methodology (MEDDPICC, SPICED, Challenger, etc.)
- Forecasting accuracy and deal inspection rigor
- Sales tech stack selection and adoption (CRM, engagement, intelligence)
- Competitive win/loss strategy and battle cards
- Cross-functional alignment with Marketing (MQL handoff) and CS (handoff/expansion)

## How They're Measured

- **Quota attainment**: % of team at or above quota
- **Pipeline coverage**: pipeline-to-quota ratio (3-4x healthy)
- **Win rate**: overall and by segment, deal size, competitor
- **Sales cycle length**: average days from opportunity creation to close
- **Rep productivity**: revenue per rep, activities per rep
- **Forecast accuracy**: predicted vs actual within 10% variance
- **CAC and CAC payback period**: efficiency of customer acquisition
- **Ramp time**: days for new reps to reach full productivity

## What Keeps Them Up at Night

- Pipeline coverage dropping below 3x with 6 weeks left in quarter
- Forecast misses that erode board and CEO confidence
- Top reps getting recruited away (takes 6+ months to replace production)
- Rep productivity declining despite more tooling and more headcount
- Deals slipping because buyers go dark or add stakeholders late
- Marketing-generated pipeline quality declining (MQLs that don't convert)
- Competitive losses accelerating in a key segment
- Sales tech stack bloat: reps using 10+ tools but none well

## Buying Triggers

- Quarter starts with pipeline coverage below target
- Quota attainment drops below 60% across the team
- Board mandates growth acceleration without proportional headcount increase
- New competitor enters the market and starts winning deals
- Major rep turnover creates an urgent ramp and productivity problem
- Company shifts upmarket (SMB to mid-market, or mid-market to enterprise)
- Post-merger integration requires combining sales teams and processes
- Sales cycle lengthening quarter over quarter without clear cause

## Language That Resonates

- "Pipeline velocity" / "pipeline coverage" / "days to close"
- "Rep productivity" / "revenue per rep without adding headcount"
- "Your reps are spending X% of time on non-selling activities"
- "Here's how [comparable company] improved win rates by Y%"
- "Works inside your CRM" / "lives where your reps already work"
- "Measurable impact within one quarter"
- "Deal intelligence" / "forecast confidence"

## Language to Avoid

- "Long-term strategic investment" (they think in 90-day cycles)
- "Change management" (implies disruption to a revenue-generating machine)
- "Training required" (reps won't adopt tools that need training)
- "Marketing alignment" as a primary pitch (not their problem to solve)
- Abstract ROI projections without real customer benchmarks
- "AI" as a feature rather than an outcome it delivers

## Best Outreach Channels

1. **Email** — short, numbers-driven, outcome-focused
2. **Phone** — sales leaders respect the phone; a well-timed cold call works
3. **LinkedIn** — engage on their wins (President's Club, quota posts)
4. **Warm intro from a peer CRO** — highest conversion by far
5. **Sales communities** (Revenue Collective, Pavilion, Modern Sales Pros)

## Best Opening Angles

1. **The rep productivity angle** — Reference their team size (visible
   on LinkedIn) and frame around revenue per rep improvement. CROs
   always want more output from existing headcount before asking for
   more budget. Quantify time saved on non-selling activities.

2. **The pipeline coverage angle** — If you can see signals of pipeline
   pressure (hiring SDRs, new outbound tools, marketing spend increases),
   connect to how your product directly creates or accelerates pipeline.
   Be specific about which stage of the funnel you impact.

3. **The competitive win rate angle** — If you know they're losing to
   a specific competitor, reference it. Share how a peer company
   improved their competitive win rate. CROs are intensely competitive
   and this angle grabs attention.

## Common Objections

- **"We already have [Outreach/Gong/Clari/etc.]."** — Don't compete
  head-on with their existing stack. Show how you complement it or
  fill a specific gap their current tools leave open. Ask what's
  still not working despite those investments.
- **"My reps won't adopt another tool."** — Agree that adoption is
  everything. Show how your product works inside their existing
  workflow (CRM, email, Slack) rather than requiring a new tab.
- **"Show me the ROI first."** — Have a concrete customer case study
  ready with numbers: pipeline generated, win rate improvement, cycle
  time reduction. CROs respect specifics, not projections.
- **"I need to see this work for one quarter."** — Offer a pilot
  scoped to one team or one segment. Define success metrics upfront.
  Make it easy to expand if the pilot works.
- **"We're restructuring the sales org right now."** — Ask about the
  timeline and new structure. Position as something that accelerates
  the new org's ramp. Restructuring is often the best time to adopt.
