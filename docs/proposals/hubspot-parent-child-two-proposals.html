HubSpot Parent-Child Company Management

Two proposals: clean up existing records, then keep them clean going forward
March 2026 — Prepared for Sean Graham & 12 Labs Team

The Problem

When contacts share a parent company email domain (e.g., @nfl.com), HubSpot auto-associates them to the parent. But many actually work for subsidiaries like the Kansas City Chiefs or Dallas Cowboys.

Today

  • Email domain is the only signal
  • @nfl.com = NFL, always
  • Chiefs employees go to the NFL rep
  • No parent-child awareness
→

After Both Proposals

  • Existing records cleaned up and properly associated
  • New contacts auto-route via LinkedIn data
  • Email domain used only as fallback
  • Edge cases flagged for human review
Two separate efforts. Proposal 1 fixes what's already in HubSpot. Proposal 2 changes how new contacts get routed going forward. They run in sequence — Proposal 1 builds the mapping table that Proposal 2 uses.
Proposal 1

Migration — Clean Up Existing Records

Goal: Take what's currently in HubSpot and move companies and contacts into the correct parent-child associations.

Step 1: Discovery & Flagging

We use Claude and Clay together to analyze existing HubSpot records and flag which companies should be parents, which should be children, and which should be merged.

SignalWhat It Tells Us
Company TypeIs this a subsidiary, division, or brand?
SegmentEnterprise vs. mid-market — helps determine if it warrants its own record
Company SizeEmployee count signals whether a child is big enough to stand alone
Unique DomainDoes the child have its own email domain (e.g., @chiefs.com vs. @nfl.com)?
Unique LinkedIn URLDoes the child have its own LinkedIn company page? Strongest signal.

For each parent company, Claude analyzes contacts and cross-references LinkedIn data, job titles, and company names to identify child companies. It outputs a recommendation: Create as child, Merge with parent, or Needs team decision.

Step 2: Google Sheet for Team Review

The output is a shared Google Sheet — one row per company — with recommendations and conflict highlights.

Example output (NFL):

CompanyContactsDetectionRecommendationConflicts
Kansas City Chiefs 12 LinkedIn URL + titles Create as child None
Dallas Cowboys 8 LinkedIn URL + domain Create as child 2 reps own contacts
NFL Films 3 LinkedIn URL Team decision Small entity — merge or keep?
NFL Network 2 Job title only Team decision Currently owned by media team rep

What gets highlighted as a conflict:

Ownership

Two reps own contacts that will move to the same child company. Who gets ownership?

Merging

A company marked for merge has activity on both records. Which record survives?

Ambiguous

Small division where it's unclear if it needs its own record (e.g., NFL Films — 3 contacts).

The sheet includes a Notes column for the team to add context only they would know. The system recommends — humans decide. No changes happen until the team signs off.

Step 3: Automated Execution

Once the team returns the approved Google Sheet, Clay handles all HubSpot updates automatically:

1

Create Children

Clay creates child company records via HubSpot API with parent-child association set.

2

Re-Associate

Moves contacts from the parent to the correct child company. Preserves all contact data.

3

Build Mapping

Creates a parent → child LinkedIn URL mapping table. This is what Proposal 2 uses.

No manual HubSpot work required. The team reviews and decides, Clay executes. Dedup protection runs before, during, and after — with a weekly scan as a safety net.

Proposal 2

Enrichment — Route New Contacts Correctly

Goal: Change the current enrichment process so that new contacts automatically land on the correct child or parent company — not just wherever the email domain points.

Today: Contact-to-company association relies on email domain (@nfl.com → NFL) and imported spreadsheet data. Both are unreliable for parent-child structures.

The Fix: LinkedIn First, Email as Fallback

When a new contact enters the system, Clay enriches them and routes based on LinkedIn data as the primary signal, with work email only as a fallback.

1

LinkedIn Company URL

Exact match against the mapping table. Most reliable — no fuzzy matching needed.

2

LinkedIn Company Name

If the URL doesn't match, check the company name. Catches subsidiaries with LinkedIn pages not yet in our mapping table.

FB

Work Email Domain (Fallback)

Only used when LinkedIn data is missing. This is today's behavior — but now intentionally last, not the default.

When in doubt, flag for a human. This doesn't need to be perfect — it needs to be better than email-only routing.

Ongoing Monitoring

Once routing is live, two automations keep it honest:

Mismatch Alert

When a contact's job title contains a known child company name but they're associated with the parent → HubSpot creates a task for the rep to review.

Re-Association Audit

Weekly report showing contacts whose company association was manually changed by a rep. High volume = routing needs tuning for that parent.

How They Connect

The Bridge: Mapping Table

Proposal 1 creates the mapping table (parent → child LinkedIn URL mappings) that Proposal 2 uses for ongoing routing. They run in sequence:

PROPOSAL 1 — FIRST

Clean Up & Build the Map

Identify parent-child relationships, team reviews and approves, Clay executes. The mapping table is the deliverable.

PROPOSAL 2 — SECOND

Use the Map Going Forward

New contacts get routed using the mapping table. As new children are discovered, they get added and routing picks them up automatically.
